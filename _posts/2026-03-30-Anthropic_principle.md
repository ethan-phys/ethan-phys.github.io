Is using our own existence as a basis for constructing physical theories merely sophistry, or can it genuinely advance the development of science?

The **anthropic principle** usually appears together with the **naturalness problem**.

There are many different formulations of the anthropic principle. The version often used for “sophistry” is: the universe must exist in its present form, because only in this form could we (human beings) exist.

This implicitly assumes a strong correlation between “the existence of human beings” and “the present form of the universe,” and at its root denies the naturalness problem in physical theory. We would then no longer need to ask questions like, “Why is the electron mass so much smaller than the tau mass?” or “Why is the cosmological constant so small?”

It may seem that the anthropic principle should not be taken seriously by physicists, yet historically there have been two “successful applications” of it. (There have been many other attempts, but these two are, in the author’s view, the most widely accepted.)

In 1952, Fred Hoyle, based on the fact that carbon-based life exists, proposed that there must exist a specific excited energy state of carbon (7.65 MeV); otherwise, this universe would not contain enough carbon. This was later confirmed experimentally, and the state is now known as the Hoyle state.

In 1987, Steven Weinberg, based on the existence of large-scale structure, proposed that the cosmological constant must be very small; otherwise, no structure would exist, and human beings would not exist either. Later observations showed that the cosmological constant is indeed extremely tiny, in striking agreement with Weinberg’s prediction.

Henry Tye believes that the essence of the anthropic principle is phenomenology: starting from the phenomena we observe, we infer what physical theory should look like. Both the Hoyle state and Weinberg’s understanding of the cosmological constant can be classified in this way. What is really being discussed here is the weak anthropic principle: replacing “the existence of human beings” with “the fact that we observe the universe in its present form,” so that its essence is phenomenological.

Tsutomu Yanagida believes that for any naturalness problem that cannot be explained by the anthropic principle, there must necessarily be dynamics behind it. On this basis, one can argue for the reality of the strong CP problem and the necessity of the existence of the axion. Although this view remains highly controversial, it can indeed point modern physics in a direction. Either one carefully studies how physical constants affect various chemical reactions in order to determine whether they influence “the existence of human beings” (that is, the phenomena we observe), or one uses large-scale catastrophic events as criteria to constrain physical constants.

String theory contains the string landscape, meaning that different compactifications of hidden dimensions can give rise to universes with different physical constants. In other words, string theory predicts “thousands upon thousands” of different realities. For this reason, most people have lost confidence in string theory, because it has lost its predictive power. Within the string landscape, can the anthropic principle guide us to the true theory of everything?

In the author’s view, the lesson the anthropic principle offers us is that we should return to phenomena. Reasonable hypotheses proposed in order to explain phenomena, together with the predictions derived from them, should be taken seriously—even when such hypotheses are bold enough to concern the destruction of the universe and the very essence of humanity.
